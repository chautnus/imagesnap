# Competitor Tracking Beyond Keyword Tools

You probably already use keyword tools — SEMrush, Ahrefs, eRank, maybe Google Trends. They show you search volume, ranking difficulty, and what terms your competitors target.

But they don't show you what competitors actually *do*.

They don't show you which products your competitor just launched, what price point they chose, how their product photography evolved over the last quarter, or what messaging they're testing on their listing pages.

That's a different layer of competitive intelligence. And it lives on product pages, not in keyword dashboards.

---

## The Gap Between Keyword Data and Product Intelligence

Keyword tools answer: *"What are people searching for?"*

Product intelligence answers: *"What are competitors doing about it?"*

Both matter. But most researchers have a solid keyword workflow and a chaotic product tracking process. Product research looks like:

- Screenshots dumped in a folder
- Bookmarks that go stale
- Notes scattered across Notion, Sheets, and Slack threads
- No way to compare product A from January with product B from March

The result: you have keyword data in a clean dashboard and competitor product data in a mess.

---

## A Practical Competitor Tracking Workflow

Here's how product researchers use ImageSnap to build a competitor database that's actually useful:

### Step 1: Define Your Fields

Before you start capturing, decide what you're tracking. ImageSnap lets you define custom context fields. For competitor tracking, a useful setup might be:

- **Competitor name** — who is this?
- **Product category** — what category does this belong to?
- **Price** — how much are they charging?
- **Date captured** — when did you see this?
- **Positioning notes** — how are they framing this product?
- **Visual style** — what's the photography/design approach?
- **Status** — new, updated, discontinued?

### Step 2: Regular Browse-and-Capture Sessions

Set a cadence — weekly or biweekly. Browse your competitors' product pages. When you see something worth tracking, click the ImageSnap extension.

In one click:
- Product images save to your Google Drive
- Page data (title, price, description, URL) goes to your Google Sheet
- You fill in your custom fields

A 30-minute session can capture 20–40 products. Over a month, you have a structured database of 80–160 competitor records.

### Step 3: Analyze in Your Sheet

Your Google Sheet becomes your competitor intelligence dashboard. Sort by date to see what's new. Filter by competitor to see their full product range. Compare pricing across competitors. Track visual trends over time.

No special analytics tool required — it's a spreadsheet with images.

## Why Not Just Use Screenshots?

Screenshots are fast. They're also useless after a week.

| Factor | Screenshots folder | ImageSnap competitor database |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Finding a specific product | Scroll through hundreds of files | Search/filter in Sheets |
| Comparing prices | Open two images side by side | Sort column in Sheets |
| Tracking changes over time | Impossible without file naming discipline | Filter by date + competitor |
| Sharing with team | Send a ZIP file | Share the Sheet |
| Adding notes | Rename the file? | Custom fields per record |

## FAQ

### Do I need to be technical to use this?

No. ImageSnap is a browser extension. You click a button. Data goes to your Google Sheet. If you can use Sheets, you can use ImageSnap.

### How often should I do competitor captures?

Weekly or biweekly works for most markets. Fast-moving categories (fashion, electronics) might benefit from weekly. Slower categories (B2B, SaaS) can work with monthly captures.

### Can my team share the competitor database?

Yes. Since the data lives in Google Sheets and Drive, you share it the same way you share any Google file — add collaborators to the Sheet and the Drive folder.

### What's the difference between this and a web scraper?

A scraper automates data collection at scale. ImageSnap is human-guided — you browse and capture what matters. For competitor tracking, the human judgment of *what's worth tracking* is often more valuable than bulk data collection.
